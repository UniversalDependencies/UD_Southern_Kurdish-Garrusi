There are verbs with 1 to 6 radicals (Schenkel 2012: 183). If all radicals are strong, the abbreviation _lit_ (literal) is used, for example _č̣ṭ_ “say” is a 2-lit. verb. If the last radical is weak, the abbreviation _inf_ (infirma i.e. weak) is used, for example _mri̯_ “love” is a 3ae-inf verb. Verbs with a reduplicated radical are labelled with the abbreviation _red_ (reduplicata) and causative verbs with the abbreviation _caus_. Finally, there are defective verbs (_Def_) which occur in one or two forms, and anomalous verbs (_Anom_) which are used irregularly compared to other verbs of their class.

### <a name="1lit">`1lit`</a>: verb with 1 radical

#### Example

* _<b>ꞽ</b>_ “say” (this is the only verb attested for the 1-lit. verb class, and it is defective)

### <a name="2lit">`2lit`</a>: verb with 2 radicals, both strong

#### Example

* _<b>č̣ṭ</b>_ “say”

### <a name="3lit">`3lit`</a>: verb with 3 radicals, all strong

#### Example

* _<b>śč̣m</b>_ “hear”

### <a name="4lit">`4lit`</a>: verb with 4 radicals, all strong

#### Example

* _<b>ptpt</b>_ “tread”

### <a name="5lit">`5lit`</a>: verb with 5 radicals, all strong

#### Example

* _<b>nṭbṭb</b>_ “sip”

### <a name="6lit">`6lit`</a>: verb with 6 radicals, all strong

#### Example

* _<b>nč̣mnč̣m</b>_ “sexual pleasure”

### <a name="2aeinf">`2aeinf`</a>: verb with 2 radicals, the last one weak

#### Example

* _<b>ti̯</b>_ “trample”


### <a name="3aeinf">`3aeinf`</a>: verb with 3 radicals, the last one weak

#### Example

* _<b>mri̯</b>_ “love”

### <a name="4aeinf">`4aeinf`</a>: verb with 4 radicals, the last one weak

#### Example

* _<b>ḥmśi̯</b>_ “sit”

### <a name="5aeinf">`5aeinf`</a>: verb with 5 radicals, the last one weak

#### Example

* _<b>nḫꞽḫi̯</b>_ “endure”

### <a name="2aered">`2aered`</a>: verb with a second reduplicated radical

#### Example

* _<b>ḳrr</b>_ “boil”

### <a name="3aered">`3aered`</a>: verb with a third reduplicated radical

#### Example

* _<b>čḥnn</b>_ “be glad”

### <a name="Caus2lit">`Caus2lit`</a>: causative verb with 2 radicals, both strong

#### Example

* _<b>ś:ḥč̣</b>_ “make bright, illuminate” (causative of the verb ḥč̣ “be bright”)

### <a name="Caus3lit">`Caus3lit`</a>: causative verb with 3 radicals, all strong

#### Example

* _<b>ś:ꜥnḫ</b>_ “make live, revive” (causative of the verb ꜥnḫ “live”)

### <a name="Caus4lit">`Caus4lit`</a>: causative verb with 4 radicals, all strong

#### Example

* _<b>ś:ꜣḫꜣḫ</b>_ “make verdant” (causative of the verb ꜣḫꜣḫ “become verdant”)

### <a name="Caus5lit">`Caus5lit`</a>: causative verb with 5 radicals, all strong

#### Example

* _<b>ś:nḫbḫb</b>_ “cause to draw back” (causative of the verb nḫbḫb “draw back”)

### <a name="Caus3aeinf">`Caus3aeinf`</a>: causative verb with 3 radicals, the last one weak

#### Example

* _<b>ś:ḫpi̯</b>_ “conduct” (causative of the verb ḫpi̯ “travel”)

### <a name="Caus4aeinf">`Caus4aeinf`</a>: causative verb with 4 radicals, the last one weak

#### Example

* _<b>ś:bꜣgi̯</b>_ “make weary” (causative of the verb bꜣgi̯ “become weary”)

### <a name="Caus2aered">`Caus2aered`</a>: causative verb with a third reduplicated radical

#### Example

* _<b>ś:ḳbb</b>_ “make cool, refresh” (causative of the verb ḳbb “be cool”)

### <a name="Caus3aered">`Caus3aered`</a>: causative verb with a fourth reduplicated radical

#### Example

* _<b>ś:bꜣḳḳ</b>_ “commend” (the verb bꜣḳḳ is unattested)

### <a name="Def">`Def`</a>: a verb used in one or two forms

#### Example

* _<b>ꞽ</b>_ “say” (this verb only occurs in the past śč̣m.n ⸗f and in the Old Semitic suffix conjugation (also called "pseudoparticiple")

### <a name="Anom">`Anom`</a>: a verb used irregularly compared to other verbs of their class

#### Example

* _<b>ꞽwi̯</b>_ “come, return”
